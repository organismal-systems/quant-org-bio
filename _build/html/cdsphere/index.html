üßÆ The Coefficient of Drag

School of Oceanography, University of Washington

As described in the previous page, the resistance force FFF on an immersed organism moving in a fluid is commonly expressed in a ratio known as the Coefficient of Drag:

Cd=F12œÅL2U2C_d = \frac{F}{\frac{1}{2} \rho L^2 U^2}Cd‚Äã=21‚ÄãœÅL2U2F‚Äã
(1)

The Coefficient of Drag is the formula used to calculate the drag forces on an organism from an observation made on a dynamically similar scale model (that is, a model that is geometrically similar and has the same Reynolds number, Re\mathcal{Re}Re).

The power of this approach is illustrated by the plot below, which shows the drag force on any size spherical organism moving at any velocity in fluid of any viscosity and density.

In this plot, note that the scales are logarithmic base 10 for both axes. Note also that there are several types of data used to define the curve:

  • cyan line (and purple dots) for experimental observations

  • blue and red lines for analytical model calculations.

The dash-dot cyan line interpolates between these data in a sensible way, providing a consistent function across many orders of magnitude of Reynolds number. The interpolation shown by the cyan line is the form in which CdC_dCd‚Äã would typically be calculated in a biomechanical investigation.

Trends in the CdC_dCd‚Äã curve¬∂

Several features of the CdC_dCd‚Äã curve are worth pointing out.

  • The denominator in this expression is very similar in form and rationale to our estimate of the wake momentum. The rationale is that, in cases where the drag forces are dominated by increases in wake momentum, the numerator will change in a roughly similar way as the denominator. By the logic in the scaling of fluid forces, wake momentum is dominant when Re‚â´1\mathcal{Re} \gg 1Re‚â´1. Therefore, we predict that CdC_dCd‚Äã will change relatively slowly at high Reynolds number.

  • In contrast, at low Reynolds number, we expect the drag from vicous forces to be much higher than from wake momentum. Therefore, we predict that CdC_dCd‚Äã will be large and (increase with decreasing Reynolds number) when Re‚â™1\mathcal{Re} \ll 1Re‚â™1.

  • In the range 104<Re<10610^4 < \mathcal{Re} < 10^6104<Re<106, the coefficient of drag has some surprising ‚Äúwiggles‚Äù. This is due to the onset of turbulence.
