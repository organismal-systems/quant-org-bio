Calculating force on a spherical particle moving at known velocity, UUU¬∂

Force calculator¬∂

This part of the worksheet enables you to calculate the force required to propel an organism of given size through a fluid with known viscosity and density. The rationale is as follows:

  1. Use the organism‚Äôs diameter, DDD and velocity, UUU, and the fluid density (œÅ\rhoœÅ) and viscosity (Œº\muŒº), to calculate Re\mathcal{Re}Re.

  2. Use Re\mathcal{Re}Re to calculate the Coefficient of Drag, CdC_dCd‚Äã,

    Cd=F12œÅL2U2C_d = \frac{F}{\frac{1}{2} \rho L^2 U^2}Cd‚Äã=21‚ÄãœÅL2U2F‚Äã
    (2)

  3. Use CdC_dCd‚Äã, the organism‚Äôs size and velocity, and the fluid density, to calculate the drag force FFF. To do this, we need to algebraically rearrange the formula for CdC_dCd‚Äã to solve for FFF.

The input panel below has text boxes for the particle size and velocity and the fluid density and viscosity as inputs, and the force required to maintain the particle‚Äôs velocity as output:

Calculating velocity of a spherical particle propelled by known force, FFF¬∂

Velocity calculator #1¬∂

As noted, the Re‚àíCd\mathcal{Re}-C_dRe‚àíCd‚Äã curve describes the movement of all spheres at all speeds in all Newtonian fluids like air and water. This is sufficient to determine the velocity of a sphere if we know its size, the force exerted on it, and the fluid characteristics.

There is however a slight complication: Both ReReRe and CdC_dCd‚Äã are functions of particle velocity, UUU, (see the equations above). However, because we don‚Äôt in general have a convenient formula for CdC_dCd‚Äã, we can‚Äôt write down an analytical formula to obtain the UUU that satisfies both these equations simulataneously.

To get past this hurdle, we‚Äôll use a time-honored mathematical technique: We‚Äôll guess.

Guessing, or more precisely developing an intelligent sequence of trial-and-error iterations, is a great way to solve many computational problems. In fact, this type of iteration is essentially what a lot of computer algorithms for solving many hard problems are doing. Below, I‚Äôve made a calculator that does this iteration for you.

Before trusting in the software, however, it‚Äôs important that you gain some first-hand experience with this iterative process. This part of the worksheet enables you to efficiently perform a sequence of iterations to determine the velocity of a particle with known force. The rationale is as follows:

  1. Guess a velocity, UestU_{est}Uest‚Äã.

  2. Use the rationale in Section 3 to calculate the force, FestF_{est}Fest‚Äã, required to propel the particle at UestU_{est}Uest‚Äã.

  3. Compare FestF_{est}Fest‚Äã to the required force, and use the error to adjust the next guess of velocity up or down as needed.

Repeat until the necessary accuracy has been achieved. With a little practice, you will be able to calculate velocity to within a percent or less error in a few iterations.

The input panel below has text boxes for the particle size, the actual force on the particle, and the fluid density and viscosity ‚Äì and your guess at the velocity ‚Äì as inputs. Its output is the force required to move the particle at the velocity you guessed.

def cd_error(r,F_D,rho,mu,U):
    """A function evaluating the error between the parameter U and the
    movement speed of a sphere with radius r subjected to force F_D in
    fluid with density rho and viscosity mu. The U for which this function
    evaluates to zero is the steady state movement speed. """
    if U==0.:
        return -F_D
    else:
        signU=np.sign(U)
        absU=abs(U)
        #print 'got here...',r,F_D,rho,mu,U
        logRe=log(2.*rho*r*absU/mu,10)
        logCd=CdRe(logRe)
        return (signU*0.5*rho*pi*r**2*U**2)*10**logCd - F_D

def U_sphere(r,F_D,rho,mu):
    """Steady-state movement velocity of a sphere in fluid, as determined by the
    root of the error metric cd_error. 
    """
    # Determine the range over which the numerical root-finding algorithm should operate:
    #Re_=2*rho*r*U./mu
    Urange=relist[-1]*mu/(2.*rho*r)
    Uerr = lambda u: cd_error(r,F_D,rho,mu,u)
    U=bisect(Uerr,-Urange,Urange, rtol=0.000001,full_output=False)
    return U
